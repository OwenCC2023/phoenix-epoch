Trade System

Main Goal: Create friction and cost for trade to appropriately account for distance to trading partners and establish a feeling of place within the game. Make location and locality matter.

General Principles:
	- Provinces are a distance from one another.
	- Sea zones are a distance from one another.
	- Air zones are a distance from one another.
	- Distances are measured mid-point to mid-point.
	- Distances take time to cross. Goods to not arrive immediately after they are shipped.
	- Effective Distances can be "shortened" through the building of infrastructure.
	- Effective Distances are "lengthened" by obstacles.
	- Domain transitions can only happen in provinces with specific buildings.
		- Sea to Land requires port.
		- Land to Air requires air cargo terminal.
		- No Sea to Air connection.
	- Only a certain amount of trade can be conducted by a given nation.
	- Trade amounts are distinguished by domain.
	- Length of a trade route proportionally effects consumed capacity.
	- Quantity of goods on a trade route proportionally effects consumed capacity.
	- Trade route length is the distance between the capitals of the two trading nations.
	- Trade always takes the shortest available path.
	- Trade can be forced to be single-domain or multi-domain.
	- Trade routes flow in one direction, and carry one good in that direction.

Components:
	- Trade capacity is generated by buildings.
	- Effective distances can be decreased by buildings.
	- Terrain impacts effective distance.
	- Each province has a terrain type (marshy, flat, rugged, hilly, mountainous).
	- Each province has a vegetation level (none, low, medium, high)
	- Each province has a temperature band (cold, mild, hot).
	- Together, terrain, vegetation, and temperature band create environment classifications (ex. mountainous, high, hot = mountainous jungle).
	- The more non-flat a province, the more extreme the temperature, and the more vegetation, the higher the effective distance to cross the province.

Tie-Ins with Other Systems:
	- Buildings:
		- Some buildings generate trade capacity in a given domain.
		- Buildings use materials that are imported by trade, and produce materials exported by trade.
	- Diplomacy (stub):
		- Trade agreements shorten effective distances between nations.
		- Tariffs increase effective distances between nations.
	- Military (stub):
		- Blockades massively lengthen effective distances between nations.
	- Policies:
		- Property Rights: More private property ownership applies a small multiplier to total trade capacity.
		- Freedom of Movement and Freedom of Association: Greater freedom of movement and freedom of association apply small multipliers to total trade capacity.
		- Market: Less regulated trade applies a small multiplier to total trade capacity.
		- Less restrictive visa policies apply a small multiplier to total trade capacity.
		

Other notes:
	- Resources are imported before the spend step and exported after the spend step. That means if there is not enough supply to export the chosen amount a lesser amount is exported. It does not trigger shortages for the rest of the economy. Imported goods are included in total supply before use-cases start to withdrawal from supply on a given turn.
	- Loss of a capital during wartime leads to all active trade-routes becoming inactive. Other nations importing or exporting goods to that country will be unable to do so until the capital is recaptured, or a new capital is established.
